About the OLF

The Oromo Liberation Front (OLF) is a political organization established in 1973 to promote the rights, self-determination, and liberation of the Oromo people — the largest ethnic group in Ethiopia and the Horn of Africa.

The Oromo constitute approximately 35–40% of Ethiopia's population, numbering over 40 million people. Despite their demographic weight, the Oromo have historically claimed political and cultural marginalization under successive Ethiopian governments, from the imperial era of Haile Selassie through the Derg military regime to more recent administrations.

The OLF emerged as a response to these grievances, advocating for Oromo self-determination — a concept ranging from cultural autonomy within a federal Ethiopia to full independence of an Oromo state known as Oromia.

The organization has undergone significant transformations since its founding, shifting between armed resistance and peaceful political engagement. It was briefly legalized and participated in the transitional government following the fall of the Derg in 1991, before returning to armed opposition after withdrawing from that process in 1992.

02

Historical Timeline

1963

Seeds of Resistance

Oromo intellectuals and activists begin organizing in response to land dispossession and cultural suppression under Haile Selassie's imperial government. The Mecha and Tulama Self-Help Association is founded, one of the earliest Oromo civic organizations.

1973

Founding of the OLF

The Oromo Liberation Front is formally established. The founding charter articulates the goal of liberating the Oromo nation from "Ethiopian colonialism." The organization adopts the Odaa tree — a sacred Oromo symbol — as part of its iconography.

1974

The Derg Takes Power

A Marxist military junta known as the Derg overthrows Emperor Haile Selassie. Despite initial land reform rhetoric, the new regime continues repressive policies against Oromo political aspirations. The OLF launches its armed struggle (the Oromo Liberation Army) against the Derg.

1976

OLA Armed Operations Begin

The Oromo Liberation Army begins significant guerrilla operations in Western and Southern Oromia. The OLF establishes rear bases in neighboring Somalia and Sudan, drawing support from regional geopolitics of the Cold War era.

1991

Fall of the Derg & Peace Talks

The Derg collapses. The OLF joins the Transitional Government of Ethiopia alongside the EPRDF, gaining recognition as a legitimate political actor. This marks the OLF's first and only period of legal political participation. Internal tensions and allegations of exclusion soon emerge.

1992

Withdrawal from Transitional Government

The OLF withdraws from the transitional government, citing harassment of its members and irregularities in regional elections. It returns to armed opposition. The Ethiopian government subsequently designates the OLF as a terrorist organization.

2000s

Exile, Fragmentation & Resilience

The OLF operates largely from exile in Asmara, Eritrea, and later Kenya and Europe. Internal divisions lead to multiple splinter factions. Despite this, the organization maintains symbolic importance and continues advocacy in the diaspora and international forums.

2018

Abiy Ahmed & New Political Opening

Prime Minister Abiy Ahmed, himself of Oromo heritage, comes to power and removes the terrorist designation from the OLF. OLF leadership returns from exile to mass celebrations in Addis Ababa. Peace negotiations begin, raising hopes for resolution.

2019
OLA Splits

OLA Splinter & Continued Conflict

A faction of the Oromo Liberation Army, now calling itself "OLA-Shane," rejects peace negotiations and continues armed operations, particularly in Western Oromia. The Ethiopian government and OLF leadership both publicly distance themselves from this faction, though it claims the OLA name.

2020–24
Ongoing Conflict

Escalating Conflict in Western Oromia

Armed clashes between Ethiopian federal forces, Amhara militias, and the OLA-Shane intensify, with significant civilian casualties reported by international observers including Human Rights Watch and Amnesty International. Peace talks stall and resume intermittently.

03

Oromo Liberation Army

The Oromo Liberation Army (OLA) is the military wing of the OLF, established alongside the political organization in the 1970s. It has undergone significant transformation and fragmentation over five decades.

⚔️

Origins & Formation

The OLA was established as the armed wing of the OLF in the mid-1970s to conduct guerrilla warfare against the Derg military regime. Drawing inspiration from liberation movements across Africa, it adopted classic guerrilla tactics in the rugged terrain of western and southern Oromia.

🗺️

Areas of Activity

The OLA has historically operated across Western Oromia (Wollega zones), Southern Oromia, and parts of Hararghe. The current OLA-Shane faction is primarily active in West, East, and Kellem Wollega zones, as well as parts of Guji zone in southern Oromia.

🏛️

Structure & Command

Historically, the OLA operated under the OLF's Executive Committee. The post-2019 OLA-Shane faction is led by a field commander known by the nom de guerre "Jaal Marroo" (Kumsa Diriba). Its exact organizational structure is not fully known, operating in a decentralized guerrilla format.

🕊️

Peace Process

Multiple rounds of peace negotiations have been reported between the Ethiopian government and the OLA-Shane, mediated by regional bodies including the African Union. As of the most recent reports, talks have not yielded a lasting ceasefire agreement, and the conflict continues to generate humanitarian concern.

📰

International Classification

The Ethiopian government designated the OLA-Shane as a terrorist organization in 2021. This designation is contested by human rights organizations who warn it has been used to criminalize Oromo civil society. The OLF as a registered political party is legally distinct from the OLA-Shane faction.

⚖️

Humanitarian Concerns

Reports from Human Rights Watch, Amnesty International, and the UN have documented serious human rights violations by multiple parties to the conflict in Oromia, including allegations against both government forces and armed groups. Independent verification remains difficult due to access restrictions.

Notable Figures

Key individuals who have shaped the OLF and the broader Oromo liberation movement across decades of political and armed struggle.

Lencho Leta

Co-Founder & Former Secretary-General

One of the founding figures of the OLF and a central intellectual voice of the Oromo liberation movement. Later broke with the OLF to found the Oromo Democratic Front (ODF), advocating for a negotiated solution within a democratic Ethiopia.

Active: 1973 – 2010s

Daoud Ibsa

Chairman, OLF (Current)

Has led the OLF through the critical 2018 peace process and the organization's return to Ethiopia. Under his leadership, the OLF was transformed into a registered political party while attempting to manage its relationship with armed factions.

Active: 1990s – Present

Kumsa Diriba

Jaal Marroo — OLA Field Commander

Known by his nom de guerre "Jaal Marroo," Kumsa Diriba is the reported commander of the OLA-Shane faction. A former member of Ethiopia's federal security forces, he joined the OLA and became the public face of the armed wing's continued resistance after 2019.

Active: 2010s – Present

Gelasa Dilbo

Former OLF Chairman

Led the OLF through a turbulent period in exile during the 2000s and early 2010s. His tenure was marked by internal organizational disputes and attempts to maintain the movement's coherence from bases in Asmara and later Nairobi.

Active: 2000s – 2010s

Jara Aba Gada

Early OLF Leader

Among the early generation of OLF leaders who shaped the organization's founding ideology during the 1970s. Contributed to articulating the Oromo self-determination agenda in international forums during the late imperial and early Derg period.

Active: 1936 – 2013

Baro Tumsa

Pharmacist & Lawyer — OLF Founding Congress Member

One of the founding congress members of the OLF. A pharmacist and lawyer by profession, Baro Tumsa was an important early organizer who contributed to building the ideological foundations of the liberation movement before his death in 1978.

Active: 1938 – 1978

Flag & Symbols

The OLF Flag

The OLF flag features three horizontal stripes representing the core values of the liberation movement. Centered on the flag is a stylized Odaa tree — the sacred sycamore fig tree that has been the site of Gadaa assemblies for centuries.

Red — the blood & sacrifices
Green — fertile land of Oromia
Black — continued struggle & resistance
🌳

The Odaa Tree

The Odaa (sycamore fig, Ficus sycomorus) is the most sacred symbol in Oromo culture. Gadaa assemblies — the traditional Oromo democratic governance system — were historically held under the shade of the Odaa tree. It represents democracy, justice, and Oromo identity.

⭕

The Kallacha

The Kallacha is a phallic ivory ornament worn on the forehead of Oromo Gadaa leaders, symbolizing authority and fertility. It is an important cultural emblem representing legitimate governance and the cycle of Gadaa generations.

🎵

Irreecha — Thanksgiving

Irreecha is the Oromo thanksgiving festival held annually at Lake Hora Arsadi near Bishoftu. It represents gratitude to Waaqa (God) for the harvest. The 2016 Irreecha festival became a flashpoint when government crowd control led to a stampede, galvanizing the Oromo protest movement.

✊

The Qeerroo Movement

Qeerroo — meaning "young man" or "bachelor" in Afaan Oromoo — became the symbol of the youth-led protest movement from 2014–2018 that ultimately helped bring about political change. The Qeerroo protest gesture (crossed wrists above the head) became iconic internationally.

Oromia Region

Oromia Region highlighted within Ethiopia

Geographic Overview

Oromia is the largest regional state in Ethiopia, covering approximately 363,000 km² — roughly a third of the country's total area. It encircles the capital Addis Ababa (known to Oromo as Finfinne) and borders nearly every other Ethiopian region.

Area 363,000 km²
Population ~60 million
Capital Addis Ababa / Finfinne
Language Afaan Oromoo
Largest City Adama (Nazreth)
Zones 21 administrative zones

Strategic Significance

Oromia is Ethiopia's agricultural heartland, producing the majority of the country's coffee — one of the world's most valuable coffee origins. It also contains significant gold and other mineral deposits, hydroelectric resources, and fertile highlands. The Oromo question — encompassing land rights, political representation, and cultural recognition — is therefore central to Ethiopia's political economy.
